Data management and warehousing for the apartment blocks management

April 1, 2020
  1. Goal of the Project:

The goal of this project is to design a comprehensive database system for the management of apartment blocks. The system aims to efficiently manage various aspects of apartment blocks, including apartments, owners, tenants, expenses, payments, and financial balances. The key objectives are as follows:

  • Data Organization: Create a structured database that accurately represents the real-world relationships and entities in apartment block management.

  • Efficient Data Management: Streamline the management of apartment details, expenses, payments, and balances to ensure operational efficiency.

  • Financial Tracking: Keep track of expenses, payments, and balances for each apartment, enabling the management of financial matters associated with apartment blocks.

  • Tenant Registration: Facilitate the registration of tenants, with a charge of $50 per year per block, and maintain their information.

  • Payment Recording: Record payments for rent and expenses (at a rate of $6 per year per apartment) to maintain up-to-date financial records.

  • Centralized Credit Collection: Enable the administration, which manages multiple apartment blocks, to centralize credit collection management. This includes identifying individuals who have not paid their expenses.

  • Classification of Owners and Tenants: Classify owners and tenants based on overdue amounts and overdue periods. This classification will help in efficiently managing credit collection and financial matters.

By achieving these objectives, the project aims to enhance the management of apartment blocks, improve financial transparency, and streamline credit collection processes for administrators overseeing multiple apartment blocks. The database will serve as a centralized hub for managing apartment-related data and financial transactions.

  1. Relational Schema

The relational schema evolved from the ER Diagram simplifies the structure by removing the generalization of “Owner” and “Tenant” into “Person” and instead maintains separate tables for owners and tenants. This separation enhances categorization and report generation.

Incorporating tenancy details within the “Tenant” table, including lease start and end dates, facilitates contract management and expense cross-checking.

Ownership information is linked to the “Apartment” table via a foreign key constraint, allowing individuals to own multiple apartments.

A three-level property hierarchy is defined with progressive primary keys for “Block,” “Building,” and “Apartment” tables. This aids in real-world identification.

Cash operations related to apartments are stored in the “Payment” table, while expenses are tracked in the “Expenses” table. Overdues and debt collection are managed through these tables.

Foreign key constraints ensure referential integrity for “Block,” “Building,” and “Apartment” relations.

Payments and expenses are linked to individual apartments. Shared expenses are calculated based on building and apartment shares.

The database records cash movements for the current fiscal year. Overdues consist of the sum of current balances and unsettled amounts from previous fiscal years, stored in the “Pre-Balance” table for invoicing.

Main operations involve tenant registration (50 per year per block) and recording rent and expense payments (6 per year per apartment). With an assumed 10 blocks, approximately 50,000 rent operations and 120,000 payment of expenses operations are expected on average.

Overall, this schema streamlines data management and financial tracking for efficient apartment block administration.

  1. SQL Query

The first query retrieves information about tenants who haven’t paid their expenses, including their SSNs, names, surnames, apartment IDs, due dates, expense amounts, and types. It filters these tenants based on lease dates and unpaid expenses.

  • SELECT Tenant.SSN,Tenant.Name,Tenant.Surname,Expenses.Due_Date, Expenses.amount, Tenant.ID_Apartment, Expenses.Type_of_expense FROM Tenant JOIN Expenses ON Tenant.ID_Apartment=Expenses.ID_Apartment WHERE Tenant.Lease_Start_Date < Expenses.Due_Date AND Tenant.Lease_End_Date > Expenses.Due_Date AND Expenses.Expense_Number NOT IN ( SELECT Expense_Number FROM Payment);

The second query identifies the owners of apartments associated with these overdue payments, providing their SSNs, names, surnames, and apartment IDs. It uses a subquery to find the relevant apartment IDs based on tenant lease dates and unpaid expenses.

  • SELECT Owner.SSN_Owner, Owner.Name, Owner.Surname, Apartment.ID_Apartment FROM Owner JOIN Apartment ON Apartment.SSN_Owner=Owner.SSN_Owner WHERE Apartment.ID_Apartment IN (SELECT Tenant.ID_Apartment FROM Tenant JOIN Expenses ON Tenant.ID_Apartment=Expenses.ID_Apartment WHERE Tenant.Lease_Start_Date < Expenses.Due_Date AND Tenant.Lease_End_Date > Expenses.Due_Date AND Expenses.Expense_Number NOT IN ( SELECT Expense_Number FROM Payment));

In simpler terms, the first query finds tenants who haven’t paid their bills, and the second query finds the apartment owners related to these unpaid bills.
